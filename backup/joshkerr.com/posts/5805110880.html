Switch to VIM?

I’m a big fan of Textmate and use it for any and all text editing on the Mac. It is a powerful editor with a big community of users who contribute useful scripts to extend its capabilities. Textmate is very much a normal editor and something that you can use with not much of a learning curve.

I have a few colleagues who use VIM, but the few times I’ve tried it I ended up uninstalling it because it is WAY too complicated. It goes against everything that I’ve been doing for the past 20 years.

Recently I’ve become more and more envious of those that use VIM and the awesome power it has to make seemly complicated tasks seem simple. I decided to try it out for a week and see if I can latch on.

About 10 years ago I decided to stop drinking coffee with lots of sugar and milk and instead drink it black. It took me two weeks of drinking black coffee before I started to prefer it that way. Now, I can’t imagine coffee any other way.

Using the cold turkey approach, I am now going to start using VIM exclusively as my text editor. I refuse to launch Textmate for anything, instead I’m going to clumsily navigate my way through VIM. By the end of two weeks I should either be signing its praises or furious about my lost of productivity. I’m guessing the former.

MacVim seems to be the logical choice for VIM on the Mac. It is a true cocoa Mac app wrapper for VIM and seems to incorporate a lot of Mac like features into it. For example, cmd-s to save. I’m also using a VIM distro of plugins and scripts called Janus which gives me a really quick and easy way to configure VIM with the most popular options. Janus is distributed on Github making it very easy to update it as more features and fixes are introduced.

This article was not edited with VIM, but I promise the next one will be.
