Tech Tool Pro now allows bootable partition

Tech Tool Pro is a long time leader in Mac repair and diagnostics. It used to be bundled with new Mac purchases as part of the Apple Care warranty.

One of the best features in the product is its ability to boot from a DVD and repair your system. Ever since the release of EFI firmware Macs, the bootable DVD hasn’t worked. Now, with the release of Tech Tool Pro version 6, you can create an e-drive bootable partition on your Mac and repair without needing a DVD.

I haven’t tried it yet, but its the logical next step in bootable diagnostics, especially considering the future of computing doesn’t have an optical media drive. Take for instance that new Macbook Airs come with media on a USB stick.

I can see problems with the bootable partition scheme though. What if you need to repair the drive with the bootable partition? Maybe you boot off a clone on an external drive?

Also new with Tech Tool Pro 6 is a cloning feature. I’m a big fan of cloning, it keeps a fully working disk on hand so that if I do have a failure, I can boot in seconds off the clone and keep on working.

I bought a copy of Tech Tool 6 and plan to take it through its paces.

Nice Tumblr workflow for the ruby user

I’ve got a new workflow to submit posts to Tumblr. When I’m not using Cortex, I’m using iA Writer and a ruby script to send my content to Tumblr without even opening a web browser.

Cortex is great for resubmitting other user’s content via the web, but when it comes to writing your own articles, forget it. This is where iA Writer comes in. This app is designed for writing, it allows you to focus on the words (think full screen editor) and it has great markdown support.

You can’t easily submit articles from a text editor (unless you’ve rigged up support in Textmate) so you need to setup a script to send your posts for you. Here is mine:

#!/bin/bash
IFS=$(echo -en "\n\b")
cd "~/Dropbox/Blog/BlogArticles/"
set `ls -t -r | tail -n 1`
echo "$1"
tumblr -c ~/.tumblrlogin "$1"

I’m using a ruby gem: —tumblr-rb— and have it setup with my Tumblr blogs username and password in my ~/.tumblrlogin file. More on the gem’s man pages about configuring the gem.

The script is simple. It looks for the most recent post in my blog article directory and submits it. In my post, I’ve got YAML config items that allow me to control where the post goes: queue, drafts, published, etc..

title: post title
type: regular
format: markdown
state: published
tags: tag1,tag2,tag3

So I write and save the posts in my blog directory and then execute my script to send them up to Tumblr. If I wanted I could rig the script to open the browser and go right to the post (the ruby gem returns the post ID after a successful post.)

I could do a lot more with it, but for now it is getting the job done.

About 10 years ago I decided to stop drinking coffee with lots of sugar and milk and instead drink it black. It took me two weeks of drinking black coffee before I started to prefer it that way. Now, I can’t imagine coffee any other way.

Drobo pro saves me from hard drive failure

I bought a Drobo Pro last year because I needed a reliable storage system. I was running a startup out of my home and I wanted not only a redundant backup solution, but something that could expand and not cost me an arm and a leg. Loosing data wasn’t an option.

Storage solutions are complicated and expensive, but Drobo is actually pretty easy. It isn’t a RAID system, but operates a lot like one except it doesn’t require you to do any difficult configuration. Simply pop in your drives, make a simple selection on how big of a disk you want, and you are done.

When I received my Drobo Pro, I configured it, copied my data to it and then completely forgot about it. It worked flawlessly and was super fast. (More on that later.)

Fast forward to now. My Drobo prompts me that there is a disk failure and my data is now unprotected. The Drobo Pro can survive a single disk failure, but two or more and you’ve lost your data. I’ve got a hot spare sitting around, so I pop it in, Drobo does its thing and then goes back into a nominal status. Everything is protected once again and there was no data loss.

There are an array of Drobo options, some cheaper than what I bought, and some more expensive. For me, the big seller on the Drobo Pro is that it uses iSCSI to connect to your Mac. In simple terms, this means I use a Cat 5 cable to connect my ethernet jack on my Mac to the ethernet jack on the back of the Drobo Pro. Now this isn’t normal ethernet, it is iSCI which uses gigabit ethernet to get speeds up to 200mb/sec. It is crazy fast, copying gigs of data or imaging my entire Macbook Pro in a fraction of the speed of even ESATA, there really isn’t anything like it.

Does holding your farts in hurt you over the long run?

In this recent publication by Ara Sahakian and his colleagues at the Gastroenterology division of the Cedars-Sinai Medical Centre in LA, they reviewed a variety of publications related to flatulence and determined that in fact the gases produced by our gut might damage the gastrointestinal tract.

Even though methane (CH4) only makes up 7.2% of the 400-1,200mL of gases we emit per day, their literature review suggested it was likely to cause the most harm, along with hydrogen sulphide (H2S). Both these gases are present in greater amounts in patients’ breath tests (yup - gas escapes through your mouth too!) who have gastrointestinal diseases, including colon cancer.

The authors were cautious to state that, “Evidence is now accumulating to suggest that methanogenic organisms and their gaseous by-products may actively participate in control of intestinal motor function,” though they emphasized, “Clearly, further investigation in this area is required.”

Mounting & unlocking two drives at startup with PGP WDE

I’ve got a Macbook Pro which I use for everything. I live in apps that use a lot of resources like Photoshop, Xcode and Powerpoint. Speed is important to me and the biggest limiting factor is my HD. So I pulled out the absolutely useless Macbook Pro DVD drive and replaced with it a HD. My boot drive is an SSD drive which means fast boot times and fast application load times. My home folder resides on the 2nd hard drive which has more space and it better suited for lots of reads and writes than a SSD drive.

This configuration is great, but makes security difficult. I want encrypted drives so that I rest assured that if I ever loose my notebook, the source code and other valuable documents that I carry around is safe. I am a PGPWDE (PGP Whole Disk Encryption) user and I’ve used WDE fairly successfully with a single drive setup, but read really bad things about using it for a dual drive setup. You see the problem is that your home folder resides on a 2nd disk that doesn’t get mounted until you enter your password after logging in. This can cause all sorts of problems with the worst of it being corruption.

Playing around with the PGP command line, I was able to find a way to not only encrypt both drives using WDE, but have them both mount after logging into the bootloader and before the OS even loads.

This setup is actually pretty simple. It takes advantage of a feature called Drive Groups for which there is no documentation anywhere. Here are the steps:

  • Encrypt your boot drive normally. Lets call this Disk 0. When it is encrypted, you should have a boot loader screen asking you for your PGP password at bootup. Now you are ready to go to the next step.

  • You need to encrypt the 2nd drive (the one with your home folder) but you’ll want to do it via the command line using the add group option:

     pgpwde —add-group-disk —base-disk (put your boot disk #) —disk (put the 2nd disk #) —auto   —passphrase ‘password’ —user ‘your name’

That should cause your 2nd disk to start encrypting. It is also instrumented to the bootloader so when you login, it is automatically decrypted and mounted.

These instructions seems to work fine for Mac OS X Snow Leopard. I haven’t tried it on any other OS, but because this is all happening before the OS loads, I assume it should work for Windows and Linux.

Vico - Mac text editor

There is a new Mac text editor in the mix and its name is Vico. Vico is the result of a personal itch from the author who wanted a beautiful modern Mac editor with the power and feature set of Textmate and VI.

Vico is clearly inspired by a lot of great software and in a lot of ways captures some of those innovations and then improves upon them. For example, Vico supports Bundles from Textmate. But rather than manually downloading them, it provides a built in plugin manager. It is so much better than how Textmate does it.

Another nice feature in Vico is the built in Explorer window. You can easily activate it via a button on the toolbar on top and make it go away when you don’t need it.

Lastly, Vico has built in SFTP support. This is actually an awesome feature because it lets me work on blog files without needing to use a separate program.

Vico is in development so you’ll need to update it with a new version every week or so. (It expires after a few days.) It should be released soon as it is very far along in its development. It works great for me.
